The concept of time value of money (TVM) is an age-old notion, later expounded upon in the 16th century by Spanish theologian Martín de Azpilcueta. The central tenet is that a sum of money holds greater value today than it would in the future.

Harvard Business School Professor Narayanan outlines three reasons for this principle:

- **Opportunity Cost:** Money held today can be invested and earn interest, increasing its value over time. By choosing to spend money now, you forgo the potential gains from investing it.
- **Inflation:** Inflation erodes the purchasing power of money over time. As prices for goods and services rise, the same amount of money will buy less in the future than it does today.
- **Uncertainty:** The future is inherently uncertain, and there's always a risk that you may not receive the money you expect in the future. This uncertainty makes money in hand today more valuable than the promise of future money.

This concept plays a crucial role in financial decision-making. It helps individuals and businesses weigh the opportunity cost of spending versus saving or investing. Interest, whether earned on deposits or paid on loans, serves as compensation for the time value of money. Investors are less inclined to spend money now if they anticipate a favourable net return on their investment in the future.

Essentially, the time value of money emphasizes the importance of considering the future implications of financial decisions. By understanding the impact of time on the value of money, individuals and businesses can make informed choices that maximize their financial well-being.

While this concept is widely accepted in conventional finance, its application in Islamic finance is subject to certain restrictions due to the prohibition of riba (interest) in Islamic law.

**Riba and the Time Value of Money**

Riba refers to any predetermined excess over and above the credit amount advanced to the borrower in relation to a specified period of time. Islamic Finance prohibits riba because it is considered exploitative and unjust, as it allows the lender to profit from the borrower's need for money without sharing in any risk or contributing to any productive activity.

The prohibition of riba raises questions about the application of time value of money in Islamic Finance. If money cannot be lent with the expectation of receiving interest, how can the time value of money be recognised?

**Islamic Finance and the Time Value of Money (TVM)**

Islamic finance addresses the TVM concept through a risk-sharing approach. Instead of lending money and charging interest, Islamic financial institutions engage in profit-and-loss sharing arrangements with their clients. This means that both parties share in the risks and rewards of the investments, ensuring that returns are generated from actual economic activity rather than simply from the passage of time.

Islamic finance also recognizes the TVM concept through asset-backed transactions. In these transactions, the value of money is linked to a tangible asset, such as a property or a commodity. The price of the asset may vary over time, reflecting the TVM concept without violating the prohibition of riba.

**Applications of Time Value of Money in Islamic Finance**

The TVM concept is applied in various Islamic financial products and services, including:

- **Murabahah:** A deferred-payment sale where the seller discloses the cost price and profit margin to the buyer. The higher price for deferred payment reflects the time value of money concept.
- **Istisna'a:** A contract for the manufacture or construction of an asset. The price of the asset may be paid in instalments, with the instalments reflecting this concept.
- **Ijarah:** A leasing agreement where the lessee pays rent for the use of an asset. The rental payments may be structured to reflect the TVM concept.
- **Sukuk:** Islamic bonds that represent ownership in an underlying asset or a pool of assets. The returns on sukuk may be linked to the performance of the underlying asset, reflecting the TVM concept.

**Conclusion**

The time value of money is a recognised concept in Islamic Finance, but its application is guided by the principles of Islamic Law, particularly the prohibition of riba. Islamic Finance institutions employ risk-sharing arrangements and asset-backed transactions to reflect the time value of money concept while adhering to Islamic principles. This approach ensures that financial transactions are fair, equitable, and aligned with the objectives of Islamic Finance.
