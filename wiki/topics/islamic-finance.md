# Islamic Finance

**Financial systems and practices compliant with Islamic law (Sharia), based on principles of risk-sharing, prohibition of usury (Riba), and productive asset-backing.**

## Summary

Islamic finance represents an alternative paradigm to conventional debt-based finance. Rooted in Quranic prohibitions against Riba (usury/interest) and Gharar (excessive uncertainty), Islamic finance structures transactions around asset ownership, profit-sharing, and genuine economic activity. Products range from deposit accounts to investment vehicles (Sukuk), insurance (Takaful), and trade financing (Murabahah). Islamic banks and financial institutions operate under both commercial prudence and Sharia compliance, verified by Sharia advisory boards.

## Core Principles

- **Prohibition of Riba (Interest):** Money cannot be a commodity generating returns solely through lending; interest is forbidden
- **Prohibition of Gharar (Excessive Uncertainty):** Contracts must be clear; speculative or excessively uncertain terms are avoided
- **Asset-Backed Transactions:** Financial products must be tied to real economic activity, not pure financial engineering
- **Profit-Sharing (Musharaka) and Agency (Wakalah):** Both parties share risks and rewards; relationships are partnerships or mandates, not creditor-debtor
- **Social Justice:** Financial transactions should promote equitable distribution and community welfare
- **Ethical Screening:** Investments avoid prohibited sectors (alcohol, gambling, weapons, pornography, etc.)

## Market & Institutions

- **AAOIFI** — Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions; sets standards
- **Global industry:** Estimated $2+ trillion assets under Sharia-compliant management (as of 2020s)
- **Regions:** Concentrated in Middle East, Southeast Asia (Malaysia), but growing globally
- **Clients:** Muslim majority countries, Muslim diaspora communities, socially-conscious investors
