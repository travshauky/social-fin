# Time Value of Money in Islamic Finance

**A framework for understanding why Islamic finance accepts time-linked pricing for assets and trade, while rejecting time-linked interest on loans.**

## Summary

Islamic finance does not treat the time value of money as a license for debt-based interest. The core distinction is between a valid sale or lease involving a real asset and a pure loan where extra payment for delay becomes interest. In this view, time can matter in pricing assets and usufruct, but not as a separate charge on money merely because time has passed.

## Key points
- The basic objection to conventional TVM in Islamic finance is that it can become a justification for riba when time alone generates extra return (from [[2026-06-28--time-value-of-money-in-islamic-finance.md]]).
- Islamic finance allows deferred pricing in valid sales, leases, and manufacturing contracts when the structure is tied to real economic activity and asset-backed exchange (from [[2026-06-28--time-value-of-money-in-islamic-finance.md]]).
- Murabahah, Salam, Ijarah, and Sukuk are cited as examples where time-related pricing can be legitimate when it reflects asset value, production, or usufruct rather than interest on debt (from [[2026-06-28--time-value-of-money-in-islamic-finance.md]]).
- The OIC Fiqh Academy has emphasized that debts should be repaid in their original currency and amount, without debt indexation that effectively turns delay into prohibited gain (from [[2026-06-28--time-value-of-money-in-islamic-finance.md]]).
