# Maysir

**Gambling, excessive risk-taking, or games of chance; explicitly prohibited in Islamic finance alongside Riba and Gharar.**

## Summary

Maysir (Arabic: ميسر, "gambling" or "ease" in an illicit sense) refers to financial transactions involving excessive speculation, gambling, or games of chance where outcomes are determined by luck rather than effort or productivity. The Qur'an explicitly prohibits Maysir (5:90–91). In Islamic finance, Maysir prohibition complements Riba and Gharar bans by ensuring that financial returns are tied to real economic activity and genuine risk-sharing, not to pure speculation or chance. Contracts with Maysir elements (one-sided bets, speculative derivatives, lottery-like structures) are void.

## Quranic Reference

> Surah Al-Ma'idah [5:90–91]: O you who have believed, indeed, khamr (intoxicants), maysir (gambling), [sacrifices to] idols, and divining arrows are, in fact, unclean from the work of Satan, so avoid it that you might succeed. Satan only wants to cause between you animosity and hatred through maysir and khamr and to avert you from the remembrance of Allah and from prayer. So, will you not desist?

## Maysir vs. Permissible Risk

**Prohibited (Maysir):**
- Pure speculation with no productive basis
- Gambling structures where one party profits solely at another's loss
- Unequal risk allocation (one party bears all risk, other only gains)
- Outcomes determined by chance, not effort or market conditions

**Permissible (Business Risk):**
- Profit-sharing partnerships where both parties share risks and rewards
- Investment in real businesses with genuine economic activity
- Market fluctuations in asset prices (Murabahah, Salam, Ijarah)
- Insurance-like structures (Takaful) where risk is pooled transparently

## Financial Instruments with Maysir Concerns

- **Futures/Options:** Purely speculative derivatives without underlying asset
- **Certain Sukuk Structures:** If returns are contingent on unpredictable events unrelated to asset
- **Forex Trading:** If based on speculation rather than genuine business need
- **Insurance Derivatives:** Non-hedging or purely speculative insurance trades
