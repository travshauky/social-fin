# Eid al-Adha

**The Festival of Sacrifice, celebrated on the 10th of Dhul-Hijjah, commemorating Prophet Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice and his obedience to Allah.**

## Summary

Eid al-Adha marks the climax of the Hajj season and commemorates a pivotal moment in Islamic history: Prophet Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his own son in obedience to Allah's command, and Allah's mercy in substituting a ram for the sacrifice (Qur'an 37:107). Celebrated globally by Muslims, it is a festival of joy, gratitude, and remembrance. The day is marked by ritual animal sacrifice (Qurbani), special prayers, feasting, gift-giving, and gathering with family and community.

## Key Points

- **10th of Dhul-Hijjah** — fixed date in the Islamic calendar
- **Commemorates Ibrahim's obedience** — recalls his willingness to submit completely to Allah
- **Global celebration** — observed by Muslims worldwide, whether performing Hajj or not
- **Qurbani (ritual sacrifice)** — animals (ram, goat, cow, buffalo) are sacrificed; meat distributed to family, friends, and the poor
- **Spiritual theme: submission** — exemplifies sincere faith and trust in Allah
- **Festival elements** — special prayers at dawn, new clothing, feasting, visiting family, gift-giving
- **Lasts 3–4 days** — extends through the Days of Tashreeq (11th–13th Dhul-Hijjah)
- **Historical precedent** — Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) was present on the first Eid al-Adha after the establishment of Islamic community in Madinah

## The Qurbani Tradition

- Performed by those financially able
- Animals must meet specific health and age criteria
- Meat is divided into three parts: family, friends/colleagues, the poor and needy
- Symbolizes willingness to give up what is dear for Allah's sake
- Reflects gratitude for blessings received
