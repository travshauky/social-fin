# Gharar

**Excessive uncertainty or ambiguity in contracts; prohibited in Islamic finance. Contracts must be clear regarding obligations, prices, and goods to be exchanged.**

## Summary

Gharar (Arabic: غرر, literally "deception" or "risk") refers to uncertainty, ambiguity, or speculative elements in financial contracts that render terms unclear or outcomes excessively risky. Islamic jurisprudence prohibits Gharar because it violates principles of fairness, transparency, and informed consent. In Islamic finance, Gharar prohibition complements the Riba prohibition by ensuring contracts are not only interest-free but also clear and equitable. A valid Islamic contract must specify: the parties, the subject matter (goods/services), the price, and delivery terms with clarity.

## Types of Gharar

| Type | Example | Why Prohibited |
|---|---|---|
| **Uncertainty in Price** | "Sell me apples at whatever price you decide tomorrow" | Buyer cannot assess value; unfair |
| **Uncertainty in Goods** | "Sell me fish in the sea" (quantity unclear) | Delivery cannot be guaranteed |
| **Speculative Outcomes** | Betting or gambling structures | Pure speculation; no productive activity |
| **Hidden Defects** | Selling damaged goods as if new | Deception violates consent |
| **Conditional Ambiguity** | "I will sell if X happens, but X is undefined" | Outcome unenforceable |

## Gharar vs. Permissible Risk

**Important distinction:**
- **Gharar (prohibited):** Uncertainty about whether the transaction will occur or terms are met (e.g., "I will sell you 100 apples if I find them")
- **Permissible Risk:** Business risk where terms are clear but outcomes vary (e.g., "I will supply you 100 apples at $5/bushel, delivery subject to harvest success")

## Islamic Finance Application

Gharar prohibition shapes Islamic products:
- **Wakalah contracts** must specify fee structure, investment parameters, and performance metrics upfront
- **Sukuk** must be backed by real assets with clear ownership terms
- **Takaful** must specify coverage parameters and claim procedures
- **Murabahah** must disclose all costs and agreed markup
